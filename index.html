The Legacy of Black Entrepreneurship

A rich history of innovation, resilience, and community building.

Early Pioneers (1800s-1900s)

Despite immense obstacles, Black entrepreneurs established successful businesses post-Civil War, including:

  • Madam C.J. Walker - First female self-made millionaire in America with her hair care products
  • Robert Reed Church - The South's first Black millionaire who built businesses in Memphis
  • Annie Turnbo Malone - Built a multi-million dollar hair care business, Poro Products
  • Anthony Overton - Founded Overton Hygienic Manufacturing Co. and the first Black-owned bank
  • Maggie Lena Walker - First woman to found a bank in the United States (St. Luke Penny Savings Bank)
  • Isaac Myers - Established a shipyard in Baltimore that employed hundreds of Black workers

These entrepreneurs not only built businesses but created jobs and economic foundations for Black communities during Reconstruction and the Jim Crow era.

The Golden Age of Black Business (1900-1960s)

Despite segregation, Black business districts thrived across America:

  • Greenwood District in Tulsa ("Black Wall Street") - A bustling center with hundreds of Black-owned businesses
  • Sweet Auburn in Atlanta - Home to the first Black-owned daily newspaper
  • Bronzeville in Chicago - Center for Black publishing, banking, and entertainment
  • A. G. Gaston - Built an empire including insurance companies, motels, and a bank in Birmingham
  • John H. Johnson - Founded Johnson Publishing Company (Ebony and Jet magazines)

These entrepreneurs created cultural and economic centers that supported Black communities during segregation.

Modern Era (1960s-Present)

Post-Civil Rights movement entrepreneurs expanded into new industries:

  • Reginald F. Lewis - First Black person to build a billion-dollar company (TLC Beatrice)
  • Berry Gordy - Founded Motown Records, revolutionizing American music
  • Oprah Winfrey - Built a media empire and became America's first Black female billionaire
  • Robert L. Johnson - Founded BET (Black Entertainment Television)
  • Janice Bryant Howroyd - Built ActOne Group, the largest privately-held, woman and minority-owned workforce management company

These entrepreneurs broke barriers in media, finance, technology, and entertainment.

Boston's Black Entrepreneurs

Boston has its own rich history of Black entrepreneurship:

  • Elijah W. Smith - Established a successful clothing store in the 1800s
  • Maria Baldwin - Pioneering educator who ran her own school in Cambridge
  • William Monroe Trotter - Founded The Guardian newspaper in 1901
  • Beth Williams - Founded Roxbury Technology Corporation
  • Darryl Settles - Restaurateur who opened Darryl's Corner Bar & Kitchen
  • Sheena Collier - Founder of Boston While Black, a membership network for professionals

These entrepreneurs contributed to Boston's economic and cultural landscape, building strong foundations for today's Black business owners.
